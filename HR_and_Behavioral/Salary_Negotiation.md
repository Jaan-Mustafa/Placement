# Salary Negotiation — Complete Tactics Guide

## The Core Mindset Shift

```
Most people think negotiation is confrontational.
It is not. It is a business conversation about market value.

Companies EXPECT you to negotiate.
Recruiters have a budget range. First offer = bottom of that range.
Not negotiating = leaving money on the table they already allocated for you.

"The offer is non-negotiable" → almost never true.
"This is our best offer" → means best offer without pushback.
```

**The single most important fact:**
> Negotiating never gets your offer rescinded. No company pulls an offer because a candidate asked for more respectfully. That fear is 100% irrational and costs people lakhs every year.

## Phase 1 — Before You Have an Offer (Information Gathering)

### Never give a number first

The first person to name a number loses negotiating power. Delay as long as possible.

```
Recruiter: "What are your salary expectations?"

Response options:

Option A — deflect:
"I'm still learning about the role and the full compensation package.
 I'd love to understand the complete picture before discussing numbers.
 What is the budgeted range for this position?"

Option B — range flip:
"I want to make sure we're aligned. What is the approved budget range
 for this role?"

Option C — if forced:
"Based on my research and experience, I'm targeting somewhere in the
 [X – Y] range, but I'm open to the full picture including equity,
 bonus, and benefits."
```

### Research your market value first

Never negotiate without data. Your confidence comes from knowing the number is right.

```
Where to research:
  → Levels.fyi           (best for tech — total comp breakdowns by company + level)
  → Glassdoor            (base salary ranges, Indian market data)
  → LinkedIn Salary      (role + location specific)
  → Blind (app)          (anonymous peer salary data, very candid)
  → AmbitionBox          (Indian market, startup + enterprise)
  → Ask peers / seniors  (most accurate — DM people with similar experience)

What to build:
  Target company: [Name]
  Role: Senior Software Engineer (L4 / L5)
  Market base range: ₹X – ₹Y LPA
  Competitor offers / market data: [list 2–3 data points]
  Your current CTC: ₹Z LPA
  Your minimum acceptable: ₹W LPA  ← know this before ANY call
```

## Phase 2 — The Offer Call

### Never accept on the call

```
Recruiter reads the offer on the phone.

Wrong response: "That sounds good, I'll take it!"
Wrong response: "That's too low." (never negotiate without thinking)

Right response:
"Thank you so much — I'm genuinely excited about this opportunity.
 I'd love to review the full written offer and get back to you.
 Can you send it over? When do you need a decision by?"
```

This gives you:
- Time to research and think
- Written details to reference
- Leverage (they want to close, time works for you)

### Ask for the deadline — then use the full time

```
Recruiter: "We need an answer by Friday."

You: "Absolutely, I'll get back to you by Friday."

→ Do NOT respond before Friday unless you're ready.
→ Silence is not rudeness — you're doing your due diligence.
→ They will not rescind for taking the time they offered.
```

## Phase 3 — The Negotiation Conversation

### The anatomy of a perfect counter-offer

```
Structure:
  1. Express genuine enthusiasm (non-negotiable — sets collaborative tone)
  2. State the specific number you want
  3. Anchor with market data / your value (not personal needs)
  4. Stay silent after asking

Template:
"Thank you for the offer — I'm very excited about this role and the team.
 After reviewing it carefully, I was hoping we could get to [X].
 Based on my research on Levels.fyi and conversations with peers at similar
 companies, [X] is aligned with the market for this level and my [Y years]
 of experience in [specific domain]. Is there flexibility to get there?"

Then STOP. Do not fill the silence.
```

**Why silence after asking is critical:**
The next person to speak gives ground. If you ask for ₹40L and then immediately say "but ₹38L is also fine" — you just negotiated against yourself before they said a word.

## The 6 Core Negotiation Tactics

### Tactic 1 — The Anchor High

First number sets the psychological anchor. Counter higher than your target.

```
Your target: ₹35L
Market range: ₹30–38L

You counter at: ₹38L

They come back: "We can do ₹34L"
You respond: "I appreciate that. Could we meet at ₹36L?"
Final: ₹36L — above your target

vs. anchoring at ₹35L:
They come back: "We can do ₹32L"
You respond: "Could we meet at ₹34L?"
Final: ₹34L — below your target
```

**Rule:** Your first counter should be your ceiling, not your target.

---

### Tactic 2 — Competing Offer (Most Powerful)

A real competing offer is the single strongest negotiating lever in existence.

```
"I want to be transparent with you — I have another offer at ₹X from [Company].
 [Target company] is genuinely my first choice because of [specific reason].
 I don't want to make this decision on money alone, but I need to be within
 range of the other offer to feel good about the decision. Is there any way
 to get to ₹Y?"
```

**Rules:**
- Never lie about a competing offer — easily verified, destroys trust, offer gets pulled
- You don't need to reveal the competing company by name ("another Series B fintech")
- Even an offer you're not excited about is leverage — you're not obligated to take it

```
What if you don't have a competing offer?
→ Go get one. Run parallel processes. Interview at 3–4 companies.
→ An offer you don't want is still worth ₹3–8L on your final offer elsewhere.
→ This is the real reason to always have multiple processes running.
```

---

### Tactic 3 — The "I Need to Feel Good About This" Reframe

Removes confrontation. Positions you as wanting to join, not as adversarial.

```
"I really want to make this work — I'm genuinely excited about the role.
 To feel good about making this move, I need to be at [X].
 I'm not trying to be difficult — I just want to make sure I'm joining
 in the right headspace. Is there any way to get there?"
```

This works because:
- You've expressed commitment ("want to make this work", "first choice")
- You've made it about your comfort, not a demand
- You've given them a way to be the hero ("is there any way")

---

### Tactic 4 — Negotiate the Full Package (Not Just Base)

When base is stuck, shift to other components.

```
Total compensation levers:
  Base salary           ← most visible, hardest to move at large companies
  Joining bonus         ← often easier to approve, one-time cost
  Annual bonus %        ← can increase target bonus payout
  Equity / ESOPs        ← negotiate grant size, cliff, vesting schedule
  Signing bonus (relocation) ← covers transition costs
  Work-from-home days   ← has monetary value (commute cost, quality of life)
  Extra PTO / leaves    ← time is money
  Early performance review ← "Can we schedule a review at 6 months instead of 12?"
  Title / level         ← affects future job searches, comp benchmarks
  Learning budget       ← courses, conferences, certifications
  Joining date          ← more time = more notice period comp from current employer
```

```
Script when base is stuck:
"I understand there may be a band constraint on base. Could we look at
 a joining bonus to bridge the gap? Or could we revisit the equity grant?
 I want to make the total picture work."
```

---

### Tactic 5 — The "Exploding Offer" Counter

When they pressure you with a deadline ("offer expires today/tomorrow"):

```
"I appreciate the urgency and I want to move quickly too.
 I'm very close to a decision — I just need 2 more days to wrap up
 a conversation I'm in the middle of so I can make the right choice.
 I don't want to commit to something I haven't fully evaluated —
 I'd be starting the role with doubt, which isn't fair to either of us.
 Can you extend to [specific date]?"
```

**Truth about exploding offers:**
- Real ones from serious companies are rare — usually a pressure tactic
- Most companies will extend 48–72 hours for a genuine candidate
- If they won't extend even 48 hours, it reveals something about the culture

---

### Tactic 6 — The "Help Me Understand" Information Gather

When they say the offer is final, don't argue — ask questions to find flexibility.

```
"I hear you — I appreciate the transparency.
 Help me understand — is the constraint on base specifically?
 Or is there flexibility in how the total package is structured?
 I want to find a way to make this work within whatever constraints exist."
```

This does three things:
- Doesn't argue with "it's final"
- Discovers WHERE flexibility exists (even if not base)
- Signals you're solution-oriented

## Handling Common Recruiter Responses

### "This is the best we can do."

```
"I appreciate that — and I believe you. I want to be transparent:
 I'm at [X] currently and the offer as structured represents [Y%] growth.
 Given what I'm bringing to the role — [specific value: experience, skill, project] —
 I was hoping to be at [Z]. Even if base is fixed, is there anything we
 can do on the signing bonus or equity to bridge that gap?"
```

---

### "What is your current salary?" (In India — often legally asked)

```
Option A — if comfortable sharing:
Share it, then immediately reframe:
"My current CTC is ₹X, but I'm targeting ₹Y based on market rates
 for this level, which is what I'd need to make this move."

Option B — redirect:
"I'd prefer to focus on the market rate for this role rather than
 my current package. Based on my research and experience, I'm targeting
 ₹Y — does that work within your budget?"

Option C — if they insist:
"My current package is ₹X all-in. To make this move worthwhile given
 my [specific growth/skill/impact], I'd need to be at ₹Y."
```

**Never lie about current salary** — background checks, payslip requests, offer letters from current employer can verify. The risk is not worth it.

---

### "We don't negotiate — everyone gets the same offer."

```
(Rare but happens, especially at some MNCs with strict bands)

"I completely understand and respect that policy.
 Can you help me understand the band for this level?
 I want to make sure I'm being considered at the right level given my experience."

→ Sometimes the fix is levelling up (L4 → L5), not moving within a band
→ Ask: "Is there any flexibility on the level I'm being hired at?"
```

## The Counter-Offer From Your Current Employer

When you resign and they counter-offer:

```
Statistics on counter-offers:
  80% of people who accept a counter-offer leave within 12 months anyway
  The reason you wanted to leave hasn't changed — just the number

Questions to ask yourself before accepting a counter-offer:
  1. Why didn't they pay me this before I threatened to leave?
  2. Have I been flagged as a flight risk now? How does that affect promotions?
  3. Did anything else change — manager, team, work, growth path?
  4. Is this a short-term retention move or a genuine re-commitment?
```

```
How to respond to a counter-offer:
"I really appreciate this — it means a lot that you value my contribution.
 This decision was never purely about money though. I need [X time] to think
 about it properly."

→ Take 48 hours minimum — never accept or reject on the spot
→ Use it as additional leverage with the new company ("My current employer
   has counter-offered at ₹X — I still want to join you, but I need the
   total package to justify the move")
```

## Scripts — Ready to Use

### Initial counter (no competing offer)
> "Thank you for the offer — I'm genuinely excited about this role. After reviewing it, I was hoping we could get to ₹[X]. Based on market data and my [Y years of specific experience], that's where I'd expect to land. Is there flexibility to get there?"

### With competing offer
> "I want to be upfront — I have an offer at ₹[X] from another company. [Target company] is my first choice for [specific reason], and I'd love to make this work. Can you get to ₹[Y] so I can make the right decision for the right reasons?"

### When they can't move on base
> "I appreciate that — could we look at a joining bonus of ₹[X] to bridge the gap? Or increase the equity grant? I want to find a way to make the total package work."

### Final close (accepting)
> "I'm really glad we could work this out. I'm excited to join and I'm committed to making a strong impact from day one. Can you send the updated offer letter?"

### Final close (declining respectfully)
> "I've given this a lot of thought and I have to respectfully decline. I have another opportunity that's a better fit for where I want to go right now. I have a lot of respect for the team and I hope our paths cross again."

## Salary Negotiation Checklist

```
Before the offer:
  □ Researched market rate on Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, Blind
  □ Know my minimum acceptable number
  □ Know my target number
  □ Know my opening counter (10–20% above target)
  □ Running at least 2 parallel processes for competing offers

When offer comes:
  □ Did not accept on the call
  □ Asked for written offer and deadline
  □ Took full time given to respond

The negotiation:
  □ Expressed genuine enthusiasm first
  □ Named a specific number (not a range)
  □ Anchored with market data, not personal needs
  □ Stayed silent after asking
  □ Explored full package (base + bonus + equity + signing)
  □ Never lied about anything

Closing:
  □ Got confirmation in writing before resigning current role
  □ Confirmed start date, title, level, all components in writing
```
